Author-s Tone, Purpose & Paraphrase

Reading questions in SNBT English often ask about the writer-s attitude, the purpose of a paragraph, or the closest paraphrase of a sentence. This means you need more than vocabulary-you need to understand how meaning is expressed. 1. Understanding tone

Tone is the writer-s attitude toward the topic. It can be neutral, optimistic, critical, skeptical, enthusiastic, concerned, or persuasive.

  • Neutral: mainly informative and balanced
  • Critical: points out weaknesses or problems
  • Concerned: shows worry or caution
  • Optimistic: highlights hope and positive potential
Remember: tone is usually revealed through word choice, not through one sentence alone. Look at the overall paragraph.

2. Recognizing the author-s purpose

Writers usually have a clear purpose. In SNBT reading passages, the purpose often falls into one of these types:

  • to inform, explain, or describe
  • to compare or contrast ideas
  • to argue or persuade
  • to warn readers about a problem
  • to evaluate a policy, trend, or phenomenon
Example: if a paragraph presents the benefits and risks of AI in schools, the purpose may be to evaluate, not simply to praise technology.

3. How to identify a correct paraphrase

A good paraphrase keeps the same meaning but changes the wording or structure. In answer choices, traps often:

  • change the meaning slightly,
  • add extreme words such as always, never, only,
  • reverse cause and effect, or
  • focus on a minor detail instead of the main idea.
Warning: do not choose an option just because it uses similar words. A true paraphrase preserves meaning, not just vocabulary.

4. Signal words and attitude clues

Certain words reveal the author-s stance quickly:

  • Positive: beneficial, promising, effective, significant
  • Negative/Critical: problematic, misleading, limited, questionable
  • Cautious: may, might, tends to, suggests
  • Strong claim: proves, clearly shows, undoubtedly
Example: -While online learning offers flexibility, its long-term effectiveness remains uncertain.- The tone is not fully negative, but cautiously critical.

5. Fast strategy for SNBT 2025/2026

  1. Read the first and last sentence of the paragraph carefully.
  2. Look for evaluative words: do they sound positive, negative, or balanced?
  3. Ask: is the writer explaining, warning, persuading, or comparing?
  4. For paraphrase questions, replace the original sentence with the option and test whether the meaning stays the same.
  5. Eliminate options with exaggerated language.
Exam habit: if two options look similar, the correct one is usually the more precise and less exaggerated choice.

6. Practice and explanation

Sentence: -Although social media can help students access information quickly, overreliance on it may reduce their ability to evaluate sources critically.-

Question 1: What is the author-s tone?

Answer: cautiously critical. The writer acknowledges a benefit, but also warns about a risk.

Question 2: Which paraphrase is closest in meaning?

  • A. Social media is completely harmful for students.
  • B. Social media is useful, but depending on it too much can weaken critical source evaluation.
  • C. Students no longer need books because social media is faster.
  • D. Critical thinking and social media are unrelated.
Answer: B, because it keeps both the benefit and the caution from the original sentence.
